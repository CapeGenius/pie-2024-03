Sprint 2 Overview

In Sprint 2 of our drone development project, we successfully completed the full assembly of the gas thruster reaction control system, which is essential for stabilizing our drone using compressed air. This sprint involved the integration of various key components that included disconnects, fittings, tubing, and an air tank fabricated from a two-liter bottle. Additionally, we incorporated a compressor and a regulating valve to effectively manage the pressurized air, culminating in a carefully designed propelling nozzle.

Nozzle Design

Completed assembly of the gas thruster reaction control system on the drone

Our project aims to design and build a system that utilizes cold gas thrusters—similar to rocket engines—to control the attitude and steering of a drone. The core components of our design include a fuel tank, a regulating valve, and a propelling nozzle, with our minimum viable product being a drone capable of self-stabilization using this thruster system.

On demo day, we plan to implement four thrusters on a stationary drone. If the drone is perturbed from its stable position, the Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) will detect the change, and the microcontroller will command the thrusters to respond accordingly to maintain stability. As an ambitious stretch goal, we aim to introduce a radio frequency (RF) component to allow for remote control of the thrusters, which would enable the drone to fly.

In preparation for this, our priorities for Sprint 2 included testing the controller software by mounting the IMU on our seesaw jig and assembling and electrically integrating the new four-thruster system. We are dedicated to our objective of delivering a fully functioning four-thruster drone for our final demo.

Seesaw simulation demonstrating the stabilization mechanism

To further validate our design choices before finalizing the mechanical and electrical components, we created a simulation that tests various parameters for the control system. This allows us to ensure robustness and reliability in our drone's stability and response mechanisms.
